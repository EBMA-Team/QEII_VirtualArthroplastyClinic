What can we learn from relevant articles

Virtual clinic reportedly saved the orthopaedic department at Frankston hospital ~$250k and 300 outpatient clinic hours (2017-2018) by shifting 30% of their appointments for total knee cases to virtual followup (Lim, Du, and Calligeros 2022).

A cohort study from the UK (Joseph, Nagy, and Fountain 2021) enrolled a combined sample of total hips and knees (n = 148) into a virtual clinic (radiographs and PROMs) and reported an average saving of £10449 per consultant per year.

Patient satisfaction after participating in a virtual clinic are also reasonably high (El Ashmawy et al. 2021), however this has yet to be tested in a randomised trial.

6.2 Related Work

The SHARKS governance report [link] shows that PROMs trajectories over time can be highly variable in the QEII population and that a demarcation could be established for patients with lower scores that should be prioritised for more intensive review, versus patients that are doing well and could be reviewed as required when circumstances change. This is apparent for both total knee (Figure 22) and total hip (Figure 24).

Additional work from the SHARKS registry has identified PROMs-based phenotypes in total knee arthroplasty cases at baseline assessment (McGill et al. 2025) that may also inform triaging of cases that could warrant different forms of follow up after hospital discharge.
